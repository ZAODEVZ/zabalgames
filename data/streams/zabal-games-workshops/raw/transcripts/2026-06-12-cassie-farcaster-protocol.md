[06:05] So enter Snapchain. Snapchain was a full rewrite and re-architecture of how the Farcaster protocol should work. Like hubs, we started with the idea that we'd be permissioned to begin with, and once we'd nailed down the issues, move to a permissionless set of validators.

[11:10] Snapchain is the existing data layer of Farcaster. It is a shard-based, Byzantine fault-tolerant chain - it is, in fact, a blockchain. There are three shards. Shard Zero is the core block engine that drives the protocol's understanding of users: who's registered, who's bought storage and how much, their name on the network, and whether they're a Farcaster Pro subscriber. Shard One and Shard Two just take your FID, divide by two, and based on even or odd you fall into bucket one or bucket two.

[12:19] There's a keying approach: when you put data on the network, there's a unique identifier for everything you do, split so it can support whatever shard model is chosen (presently divide by two), but amenable to alternative shard designs to spread data across more nodes. Presently a full validator node replicates Shard Zero, One, and Two's blocks - to be a validator you're basically running a blockchain with extra steps; it's technically three blockchains that roll into one. Read validators have flexibility - they could care about only one shard - but I don't think anybody's configured it that way.

[13:28] Hypersnap fits in as an additive layer. We're keen about not disrupting the network - everything we do adds to rather than conflicts with it. Hypersnap runs what is effectively a parallel network, or in classical blockchain parlance, a side chain. We do consensus a little differently - it's still BFT, but we do a special MPC (multi-party compute) protocol to construct a threshold ECDSA key. ECDSA is the signing algorithm used by Bitcoin, Ethereum, and all the EVM chains. When your wallet asks you to sign for something, you're creating an ECDSA signature.

[14:43] We chose this because everything validated on the network, we want to relay to other networks. For Ethereum this matters for the Snap token. We use Verkle trees in some places - more of an implementation detail, but relevant because it's a future roadmap item for Ethereum, so we're already aligned with the future of Ethereum. The difference: a Merkle tree changes its hash when you mutate it, and a proof means walking every step up the tree and giving those hashes - the bigger the tree, the bigger the proof. A Verkle tree proof is constant size: you give two small point values and prove anything. But on Ethereum today a Verkle proof is very expensive in gas, so for now we still use a Merkle tree for things proven for the bridge.

[16:24] We're basically a side chain, so the Hypersigner messages (an envelope over the messages sent for consensus) never enter Snapchain. If you're a Snapchain node talking to a Hypersnap node, you just think you're talking to a Snapchain node, because the node knows not to talk anything non-Snapchain to you - again, we're not trying to break the network. The other substantial component is that it adds the Snap token, which supports confidential transfers, a bridge, proof-of-work scoring for consensus, the mini app registry, node attestations, and more.

[16:55] On identities and names, there are external anchors important regardless of which side of the protocol you're on. [18:32] The key registry is how a signer is produced. You have your FID and a custody wallet that owns it - your internal Ethereum wallet, the seed phrase you use to log back in. [20:16] So the protocol was extended with gasless signers - an FIP I proposed right after the [Skedley] compromise, where [Rish]'s signer was compromised and was promoting a clanker scam token. [22:18] On the Hypersnap side there's one contract to be concerned with: the Snap token. There's a specific amendment to otherwise standard ERC-20 contracts called Hypersnap Bridge, which lets you wrap the Snap token onto the L1. This is where the MPC-based signer approach works: whenever the protocol has a new block, any transfers and bridge events are encoded in that Merkle root, and that root is signed by the quorum of Hypersnap validators. That message is instantly relayable - if you bridge a token onto Eth mainnet, Base, or any L2, the signed output of each block is freely transferable to the contract, so you get gasless bridging.

[23:48] We use standard OpenZeppelin-based contracts for the ERC-20, a proxy-based deployment so we can respond rapidly for security. [25:12] Under Snapchain's architecture, the consensus mechanism is Malachite - a Rust implementation of Tendermint. Being basic BFT means if over one-third of validators are offline, broken, or malicious, the network halts. But it's blindingly fast, which has led to Snapchain's one-second block times. I've recommended to the Neynar team what they need to do when extending geographies: the shorter your block time, the harder consensus is when nodes are further apart. The speed of light does not care about your ambitions - a full round trip between three equidistant peers needs at least 250 milliseconds, and with more nodes you deal with point-to-point latencies. I recommended three seconds; they may decide differently.

[27:03] Shard Zero is the block proposer that binds everything together; the shard proposers are the individual shards. Every validator covers all of them in parallel. There's the standard propose-vote-commit-decide approach of Tendermint consensus, and read validators - the majority of nodes - simply verify, rederive, confirm alignment, commit the block, and everyone's happy. Snapchain's storage layer is a RocksDB instance with parameters tuned after memory profiling. There are per-FID stores: the cast store, the reaction store (likes and recasts), links (people you follow), user data store (profile data), verification store (your verified addresses - that's why you see someone with six ETH addresses and a Solana address), and recently the signer store for gasless signers. It's all based on a Merkle tree, because Snapchain is an extension of the original hub architecture, which made migrating the data over easy - or so we thought. [29:47] Hypersnap architecture is an actor-driven model - we have a lot more actors at play. Actors (in the programming sense) are autonomous processes that speak to one another. We have a hyper actor running alongside the Snapchain consensus loop, with inbound events from Gossip that let the Hypersnap side protocol communicate and coordinate - rotating keys, signing new blocks. Queues run in both directions with a standardized message format, split onto dedicated topics so a node that doesn't need something (maybe it's not a Hypersnap validator, just ingesting data) can skip it. Per actor there's at most one distributed key generation (DKG) ceremony, which enables that ECDSA signature, and one signature ceremony producing the migration of keys and the output block headers used for bridging.

[31:34] We extend the state machine with a hyperblock index - similar to the Snapchain index, except a Verkle tree that lets us post KZG blobs (not yet, but we can - important as Ethereum grows). We have per-epoch shares of the key material (each validator holds one) and per-epoch DKLS group address registries to guarantee rotation between epochs. Epochs note who's coming in or leaving as a validator and any misbehavior. For the Snap token we have a note store - think a hybrid of Monero and Zcash; it's the same approach we use for Quilibrium's confidential transactions - the core principle being that you as sender and the recipient can be aware of who's sending to whom, but the rest of the network shouldn't have to be.

[32:58] There's a reward store for running a validator, a trust score store (our decentralized way of identifying spammers), and a fingerprint store for proof of quality of messages. On any social app sometimes people repeat the same post because it's funny - if you're well-established that shouldn't impact you - but we have bad actors who spam wallet-drainer mini apps or the same message repeatedly. ([Rish], Dan, Horse Facts and a few others were getting tag-blasted yesterday by someone with sock-puppet accounts demanding they talk on Telegram.) That spam gets negated on Hypersnap because we look at the fingerprints of messages: low trust score plus a spammy fingerprint means a fee. We have our own validator registry for the work that happens on Hypersnap; epochs organize meaningful changes to the validator set, and per epoch a DKG ceremony splits the new key to each member, a threshold of two of N over three plus one, mirroring the BFT consensus. Validator scoring ties into user scoring so chronically offline or misbehaving nodes can be eliminated without headaches for the network.

[35:21] Block production on Hypersnap ticks every block time - 12 seconds (I mistyped three earlier). The proposer is derived from the active validator set plus missed-proposal tracking. Construction is typical: take messages from a mempool, apply confidential transfers and locks to the Merkle tree to ensure validity, snapshot the state, present it as the root for the block, build the envelope, get consensus. We have our own gossip topics for a few message types: hypersigner messages (off-chain signer registration), validator events (register/unregister per epoch), confidential lock body messages (the actual Snap token transfers), the hyperlock Merkle-root update (how the bridge functions), the hyper owner rotation (rotating validator-set members each epoch), inbound burn (bridging back to Hypersnap), plus bookkeeping: reward issuance, trust snapshot update, node attestation, app usage receipt, and mini app registration.

[37:33] On the bridge: outbound is confidential - nobody knows the amounts until you hit the bridge (which has to indicate how much, or you couldn't mint). Inbound processing handles the L1 burn events: a relayer submits the burn, we verify it happened, and the owner of the key that burned is the one able to bridge it back. For defense in depth, we have Byzantine fault-tolerant threshold derivation so no monolithic actor can change things. Look at how LayerZero's bridge worked - in the [Kelp DAO] hack it was a one-of-one validator, the worst thing possible for a bridge. We avoid that because the validators of Hypersnap are also the quorum of signers that enables the bridge.
