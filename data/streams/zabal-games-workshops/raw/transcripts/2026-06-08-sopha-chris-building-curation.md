Sopha's not trying to replace that. Sopha is an intentional third place - a coffee shop, a library, a cultural center - for when you want to sit down, focus, and get into the really interesting stuff. What makes Farcaster interesting is the people on it, and this captures the deeper thoughts, the longer takes.

It came about seven or eight months ago when Farcaster was going to shit in a meme coin. Everything was about tokens. Nothing wrong with tokens, but it was destroying the core experience I was there for - hanging out with interesting thinkers, artists, builders, and makers. I kept thinking, "If only someone would build a client focused on depth," and nobody did. At some point I went, "Maybe now that we've got really decent vibe coding, I'm the person in the room who needs to do this." It became a sort of "let's save Farcaster" mission from a cultural perspective. Then Neynar took over around January and Farcaster started heading the direction I'd hoped for, so the urgency eased - but the heart of it is still bringing together high-signal people, high-signal writing, and high-signal art.

The two core things: a weekly community call, open and fluid, about this tech-enabled future and how we live well in it; and the client, a curated feed of high-signal writing on Farcaster. It's curated by everyone in the Sopha community who has a curator role - straightforward to get: engage with me and show you'll make a positive contribution with taste and judgment that fits. BradQ runs a dedicated feed called The Pulse, the key Farcaster moments.

Chris: Farcaster is a wild, fascinating experiment. For Sopha, I'm building for a bigger community of like-minded people interested in depth, in how to live well, in what this technology enables, and how we come together in this scenius.
